NREMT Progressive Case Study #1

Topic: Difficulty Breathing

Step 1 – Initial Dispatch

You are dispatched at 1430 for a 62-year-old male having difficulty breathing. Family states he "can't catch his breath."

You arrive to find the patient sitting upright in a recliner. He is speaking in short sentences.

Assessment

  • Awake and oriented
  • Skin cool and pale
  • Respiratory rate 30/min
  • Audible wheezing
  • Pulse 118
  • Radial pulses present

Question 1: What should the EMT do FIRST?

  • A. Assist with the patient's albuterol inhaler
  • B. Apply high-flow oxygen and perform a primary assessment
  • C. Obtain a SAMPLE history
  • D. Place the patient on the stretcher

Step 2 – More Information

After oxygen is applied, you obtain additional information.

Medical History: CHF, Hypertension, Diabetes

Family states he became short of breath while watching television.

BP: 178/96
Pulse: 118
Respirations: 30
SpO₂: 86%

Lung sounds: Crackles at both bases, Mild wheezing

Question 2: These findings MOST strongly suggest:

  • A. Asthma
  • B. Pulmonary edema
  • C. COPD exacerbation
  • D. Pneumonia

Step 3 – Patient Worsens

While preparing for transport, the patient becomes more anxious.

He now presents with:

  • Pink frothy sputum
  • Increasing crackles
SpO₂: 82%
Respirations: 36

Question 3: Which intervention is MOST appropriate?

  • A. Encourage the patient to lie flat
  • B. Assist with albuterol
  • C. Apply CPAP if available and indicated
  • D. Delay transport for additional assessment

Step 4 – En Route

Five minutes into transport, you reassess the patient.

Patient reports less shortness of breath.

BP: 168/90
Pulse: 122
SpO₂: 91%
Respirations: 26

Question 4: What indicates your treatment is effective?

  • A. Heart rate increased
  • B. Blood pressure remains elevated
  • C. Oxygen saturation improved and work of breathing decreased
  • D. Patient is still anxious

Step 5 – Hospital Arrival

You arrive at the emergency department. The receiving nurse asks for your turnover report.

Question 5: Which information is MOST important to include?

  • A. The patient watches television every afternoon.
  • B. The patient has diabetes.
  • C. Initial respiratory distress, crackles, pink frothy sputum, CPAP response, and trending vital signs.
  • D. The family called 911 immediately.

Case Study Complete

Great job! You have successfully managed a patient with acute pulmonary edema.

Key Takeaways for the NREMT:

  • Primary Assessment: Always address life-threats (Airway, Breathing, Circulation) first. High-flow O2 is critical for respiratory distress.
  • Pulmonary Edema: History of CHF + crackles + hypertension + pink frothy sputum = Pulmonary Edema.
  • CPAP: Continuous Positive Airway Pressure is the gold standard for conscious patients with pulmonary edema to push fluid out of the alveoli.
  • Trending: Monitoring the response to treatment (improved SpO2, decreased work of breathing) is vital for reassessment.
  • Reporting: A professional turnover focuses on the chief complaint, interventions, and patient response.

NREMT Progressive Case Study #3

Topic: Cardiac Emergency (Suspected ACS)

Step 1 – Initial Dispatch

You are dispatched at 10:15 for a 55-year-old male complaining of sudden onset chest pain. His wife states he "just grabbed his chest and said it felt like an elephant was sitting on him."

You arrive to find the patient sitting on the living room floor, clutching his chest. He appears pale and diaphoretic.

Assessment

  • Awake, anxious, and responsive to verbal stimuli.
  • Skin cool, pale, and diaphoretic.
  • Respiratory rate: 20/min, unlabored.
  • Pulse: 100/min, regular, strong radial pulses.
  • Complains of 8/10 crushing chest pain radiating to his left arm and jaw.

Question 1: What is the EMT's FIRST priority after ensuring scene safety and initial patient contact?

  • A. Administer oxygen via nasal cannula.
  • B. Obtain a full set of vital signs.
  • C. Administer aspirin and assist with nitroglycerin (if prescribed and indicated).
  • D. Perform a rapid transport to the nearest hospital.

Step 2 – More Information

After administering aspirin (324mg chewable) and confirming the patient does not have a prescribed nitroglycerin, you obtain additional information.

BP: 140/90
Pulse: 98/min
Resp: 18/min
SpO₂: 96%
Glucose: 105 mg/dL

History

  • Medical History: Hypertension, Hyperlipidemia.
  • Medications: Lisinopril, Atorvastatin.
  • Allergies: Penicillin.
  • Events: Pain started suddenly while reading the newspaper.
  • Family History: Father died of a heart attack at 60.

Question 2: Based on the patient's presentation, vital signs, and medical history, what is the MOST likely underlying cause of his chest pain?

  • A. Anxiety attack
  • B. Musculoskeletal injury
  • C. Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)
  • D. Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD)

Step 3 – Patient Condition Changes

While preparing for transport, the patient's pain level increases to 9/10. He becomes more agitated and short of breath.

New Assessment Findings:

  • Respiratory rate: 24/min, shallow.
  • SpO₂: 92% on room air.
  • New onset of mild crackles in both lung bases.
  • BP: 150/96 mmHg.

Question 3: Which intervention is MOST appropriate at this point?

  • A. Administer a second dose of aspirin.
  • B. Apply high-flow oxygen via non-rebreather mask.
  • C. Encourage the patient to stand and walk to the ambulance.
  • D. Delay transport to obtain a 12-lead ECG.

Step 4 – En Route

Five minutes into transport, after high-flow oxygen has been applied, the patient reports his pain has decreased to 6/10. He is still anxious but his breathing is less labored.

BP: 130/88
Pulse: 90/min
Resp: 20/min
SpO₂: 98% (on NRB)

Question 4: What indicates your treatment is effective?

  • A. The patient's blood pressure has decreased.
  • B. The patient is still anxious.
  • C. Improved SpO₂ and decreased subjective pain and work of breathing.
  • D. The pulse rate has decreased.

Step 5 – Hospital Arrival

You arrive at the emergency department. The receiving physician asks for your report.

Question 5: Which information is MOST important to include in your verbal report?

  • A. The patient's wife was very worried.
  • B. The patient's last oral intake was breakfast 2 hours ago.
  • C. Chief complaint, onset time, initial assessment findings, interventions performed (aspirin, oxygen), patient's response to treatment, and trending vital signs.
  • D. The patient has a family history of heart disease.

Case Study Complete

Great work. You have successfully managed a suspected Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS) case.

Learning Objectives Recap:

  • ACS Recognition: Crushing chest pain, radiation to jaw/arm, and diaphoresis are hallmarks of cardiac compromise.
  • Priority Interventions: Administering aspirin early is a critical, life-saving step for suspected ACS.
  • Airway & Oxygen: High-flow oxygen is essential when signs of respiratory distress or hypoxia (SpO₂ < 94%) develop.
  • Reassessment: Continually monitoring pain levels and work of breathing is vital for gauging treatment efficacy.
  • Handoff Report: Focusing on onset, interventions, and trends ensures a seamless transition of care.
NREMT Competencies: Cardiac Emergencies, Pharmacology (Aspirin), Patient Assessment, EMS Communication.

NREMT Progressive Case Study #2

Topic: Neurologic Emergency (Suspected Stroke)

Step 1 – Initial Dispatch

You are dispatched at 0815 for a 71-year-old female with sudden weakness. Family reports she "started acting strange about 20 minutes ago."

You arrive to find the patient sitting on the couch. She is awake but appears confused.

Initial Assessment

  • Airway is patent
  • Breathing is adequate (Rate: 18/min)
  • Pulse: 92/min, regular
  • Skin warm and dry
  • Findings: Slurred speech and weakness in her right arm.

Question 1: What should the EMT do FIRST?

  • A. Help the patient walk to the ambulance.
  • B. Perform a rapid neurologic assessment using a stroke scale.
  • C. Give the patient aspirin.
  • D. Obtain a detailed medical history before continuing.

Step 2 – More Information

You perform a stroke assessment and gather more details.

Onset: Symptoms began suddenly while eating breakfast, approximately 25 minutes ago.

Assessment Findings:

  • Right facial droop
  • Right arm drifts downward
  • Slurred speech
BP: 188/96
Pulse: 90/min
Resp: 18/min
SpO₂: 96%
Glucose: 112 mg/dL

Question 2: These findings MOST strongly suggest:

  • A. Hypoglycemia
  • B. Acute ischemic stroke
  • C. Syncope
  • D. Seizure

Step 3 – Patient Condition Changes

While preparing for transport, the patient's condition changes.

New Findings:

  • Increasing difficulty speaking
  • Unable to move the right leg

Airway remains open and respirations remain adequate.

Question 3: What is the MOST appropriate next action?

  • A. Delay transport while repeating a complete physical exam.
  • B. Begin immediate transport to the nearest appropriate stroke center while continuing reassessment.
  • C. Encourage the patient to drink water.
  • D. Allow the patient to rest until symptoms improve.

Step 4 – En Route

During transport, the patient vomits once and becomes drowsy.

Reassessment:

  • Airway: Partially obstructed by vomit
  • Respirations: 20/min
  • Pulse: 96/min

Question 4: What should the EMT do FIRST?

  • A. Insert an oropharyngeal airway immediately.
  • B. Suction the airway and protect it while maintaining spinal alignment as indicated.
  • C. Give oral glucose.
  • D. Reassess blood pressure before treating the airway.

Step 5 – Hospital Arrival

You arrive at the stroke center. The physician asks for your report.

Question 5: Which piece of information is MOST important to include?

  • A. The patient skipped breakfast yesterday.
  • B. The patient's exact time symptoms began, neurologic findings, blood glucose result, and changes during transport.
  • C. The patient usually watches television in the morning.
  • D. The patient has high blood pressure.

Case Study Complete

Excellent work. You successfully recognized and managed a time-sensitive neurologic emergency.

Learning Objectives Covered:

  • Stroke Recognition: Identifying sudden unilateral weakness, facial droop, and speech difficulty.
  • Stroke Scale: The importance of using validated assessment tools early.
  • Differential Diagnosis: Using blood glucose to rule out hypoglycemia.
  • Urgency: Prioritizing rapid transport to a specialized stroke center.
  • Airway Protection: Managing complications like vomiting in a patient with decreased level of consciousness.
  • Communication: Providing a critical report focusing on "Last Known Well" time.
NREMT Competencies: Neurologic Emergencies, Primary Assessment, Airway Management, Medical Decision-Making.

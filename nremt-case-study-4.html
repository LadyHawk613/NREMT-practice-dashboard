NREMT Progressive Case Study #4

Topic: Cardiac Emergency (Cardiogenic Shock)

Step 1 – Initial Dispatch

You are dispatched at 13:00 for a 68-year-old male experiencing severe chest pain and altered mental status. His daughter states, "My dad has been complaining of chest pain all morning, and now he's really confused and barely responding."

You arrive to find the patient lying supine on the couch. He is lethargic but opens his eyes to verbal stimuli. His skin is cool, pale, and clammy.

Assessment

  • Level of Consciousness: Lethargic, opens eyes to verbal stimuli.
  • Airway: Patent, but shallow respirations.
  • Breathing: Respiratory rate 24/min, shallow.
  • Circulation: Radial pulses weak and rapid. Capillary refill time > 3 seconds.
  • Skin: Cool, pale, clammy.
  • Chief Complaint: Severe chest pain prior to altered mental status.

Question 1: What is the EMT's MOST immediate concern and intervention for this patient?

  • A. Administer 324 mg of aspirin.
  • B. Obtain a detailed medical history from the daughter.
  • C. Assess for signs of hypoperfusion and apply high-flow oxygen.
  • D. Prepare for rapid transport to the nearest cardiac center.

Step 2 – More Information

After applying high-flow oxygen via non-rebreather mask, you obtain a set of vital signs and further history.

BP: 80/50
Pulse: 120/min
Resp: 28/min
SpO₂: 90% (on NRB)
Glucose: 130 mg/dL

History

  • Medical History: Previous Myocardial Infarction (MI) 2 years ago, Hypertension, Type 2 Diabetes.
  • Medications: Metoprolol, Aspirin, Lisinopril, Metformin.
  • Events: Chest pain started 3 hours ago, confusion developed in the last hour.

Question 2: Based on these findings, what is the MOST likely diagnosis for the patient's current condition?

  • A. Hypoglycemic emergency
  • B. Anaphylactic shock
  • C. Cardiogenic shock
  • D. Septic shock

Step 3 – Patient Worsens

While preparing for transport, the patient becomes increasingly dyspneic. You notice frothy sputum at the mouth and hear diffuse crackles upon auscultation of the lungs.

New Assessment Findings:

  • Respiratory rate: 32/min, severe accessory muscle use.
  • SpO₂: 85% on non-rebreather mask.
  • Frothy sputum present.
  • Diffuse bilateral crackles in lung fields.

Question 3: Which intervention is MOST appropriate given the patient's deteriorating respiratory status?

  • A. Administer another dose of aspirin.
  • B. Initiate positive pressure ventilation (e.g., CPAP or BVM with PEEP) if available and indicated.
  • C. Elevate the patient's legs to improve blood flow to the brain.
  • D. Administer oral glucose to improve mental status.

Step 4 – En Route

En route to the hospital, you initiate CPAP at 10 cmH₂O. After 5 minutes, the patient's condition shows slight improvement.

BP: 85/55
Pulse: 110/min
Resp: 26/min
SpO₂: 92% (on CPAP)

Patient remains lethargic but is more responsive to verbal commands.

Question 4: What is the MOST important aspect of ongoing management for this patient during transport?

  • A. Continuously monitor vital signs and reassess respiratory and circulatory status.
  • B. Administer a fluid bolus to increase blood pressure.
  • C. Prepare for administration of intravenous nitroglycerin.
  • D. Encourage the patient to cough forcefully to clear secretions.

Step 5 – Hospital Arrival

You arrive at the cardiac catheterization lab. The interventional cardiologist asks for your report.

Question 5: Which information is MOST critical to convey to the receiving physician?

  • A. The patient's daughter was very upset.
  • B. The patient's exact last oral intake.
  • C. Onset of chest pain and altered mental status, history of MI, signs of shock and pulmonary edema, interventions (oxygen, CPAP), and patient's response.
  • D. The patient's home medications.

Case Study Complete

Excellent work. You have successfully managed a critical case of Cardiogenic Shock.

Learning Objectives Recap:

  • Shock Recognition: Hypotension (BP < 90), tachycardia, and altered mental status are critical signs of shock.
  • Cardiogenic Etiology: History of MI combined with signs of pump failure (pulmonary edema) points to Cardiogenic Shock.
  • Positive Pressure Ventilation: CPAP or BVM with PEEP is vital for managing acute pulmonary edema by forcing fluid out of the alveoli.
  • Fluid Management: Be cautious with fluids in cardiogenic shock, as they can exacerbate pulmonary edema.
  • Continuous Reassessment: Unstable patients require constant monitoring of ABCs and vital signs during transport.
NREMT Competencies: Shock Management, Cardiac Emergencies, Airway Management (PPV), EMS Communication.
